### 1. What is AI governance?

```
AI governance is the set of frameworks, rules, and processes surounding ai.
```

### 2. Why is AI governance considered a socio-technical problem rather than purely a technical problem?

```
Ai is developed and deployed within teams and societies of people, mostly as a tool to be used. Problems can stem not only from technical details but also how the model is used, shared, and viewed by the people in the group/
```

**Feedback:**
> Captures the essence well. Could strengthen by mentioning specific alignment areas (people, process, technology, risk, legal, compliance).

**Marks: 4/5**

---

### 3. What business risks can arise from poorly governed AI systems?

```
* reputation risk
* legal risk
* privacy
* copyright
* bias
```

**Feedback:**
> Missing: hallucinations, cyber vulnerabilities, model drift, regulatory exposure, operational failures — the handbook lists 9 distinct risks.

**Marks: 2/5**

---

### 4. Why is AI governance especially important in regulated industries like banking?

```
Banking deals with a lot of sensitive customer information. Pins, passwords, financial information, pii. The non deterministic nature magnifies the risks already associated with traditional software. Furthermore, regulated industries face intense scrutiny from external legal regulators and could face sever fines for violating regulation.
```

**Feedback:**
> Well-reasoned for banking context. Could mention specific regulatory scrutiny (e.g., ECB, FCA, GDPR, AI Act).

**Marks: 4/5**

---

### 5. What is the difference between AI governance and AI security?

```
Ai security is about preventing breaches from bad actors or badly designed systems. Governance is about making sure organisations use ai responsibly.
```

**Feedback:**
> Captures high-level distinction but misses the detailed relationship — the handbook shows they cover different concerns (ethics, explainability, accountability vs. confidentiality, integrity, incident response).

**Marks: 3/5**

---

### 6. Why are trust and explainability important in enterprise AI systems?

```
We need to be able to say why a model made a certain decision in order to have confidence in that decision and be able to justify it to regulators/customers.
```

**Feedback:**
> Covers trust, explainability, and auditability well.

**Marks: 4/5**

---

### 7. What are "guardrails" in AI systems?

```
Guardrails are measures put in place to ensure the system is behaving as you expect.
```

**Feedback:**
> Technically correct but could be more specific — the handbook frames this in terms of policies, controls, and monitoring.

**Marks: 3/5**

---

### 8. Why is human oversight important in enterprise AI?

```
Becaus the inherent unpredictability of ai models, human oversignt is key in high risk areas to minimise exposure.
```

**Feedback:**
> Missing the handbook's key points: humans must be able to review, intervene, and override outputs — it's about maintaining control, not just oversight.

**Marks: 2/5**

---

### 9. What is meant by accountability in AI governance?

```
Someone is responsible for the outcome.
```

**Feedback:**
> The handbook emphasizes named accountability, documented responsibility, governance ownership, escalation authority — vague ownership is flagged as a major governance failure mode.

**Marks: 1/5**

---

### 10. Why is "everyone owns AI" often considered a governance anti-pattern?

```
"When everyone is super, no one will be"
```

**Feedback:**
> Captures the consequence correctly — the handbook explicitly calls "everyone owns AI" a common governance anti-pattern alongside "nobody owns AI" and "we don't use AI".

**Marks: 4/5**
